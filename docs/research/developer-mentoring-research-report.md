## 1. Mentoring Findings

### What Good Mentoring Looks Like

**Core Practices** [https://www.pluralsight.com/resources/blog/cloud/how-to-be-a-good-mentor-in-software-engineering]

- **Clear Expectations & Goal-Setting**: Establish specific, measurable objectives with mentees (e.g., mastering a language, improving code review skills).
- **Active Listening**: Listen without interrupting; foster an environment where no question is dismissed as "dumb."
- **Personalized Learning Paths**: Assess individual strengths/weaknesses and adapt mentoring style and task assignments accordingly.

**Practical Techniques** [https://www.codementor.io/blog/mentoring-guide-for-developers-8z8y984usf]

1. **Pair Programming** [https://par.nsf.gov/servlets/purl/10281045]
   - Write code together; alternate between "driver" (writing code) and "navigator" (reviewing/suggesting) roles.
   - Provides immediate, real-time feedback.
   - Research shows pair programming enhances code quality and enjoyment, particularly when both partners are equally engaged.

2. **Code Reviews with Explanation**
   - Go beyond comments: highlight patterns, best practices, and common pitfalls.
   - Occasionally ask mentees to review your code to build confidence and reverse the learning flow.

3. **Guided Problem-Solving** [https://martinfowler.com/articles/mentoring.html]
   - Ask leading questions rather than providing direct solutions.
   - Teach how to find answers (documentation, Stack Overflow, etc.).
   - This builds resourcefulness and independent thinking.

4. **Regular Check-ins**
   - Weekly or biweekly cadence.
   - Track progress with checklists, milestones, or shared documents.

5. **Exposure to Broader Context**
   - Invite mentees to architecture/design discussions.
   - Connect them with others inside and outside the team.

6. **Safe Learning Environment**
   - Celebrate mistakes as learning opportunities [https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1140923.pdf]
   - Maintain psychological safety; promote growth mindset.
   - This is critical for retaining underrepresented groups in computing [https://par.nsf.gov/servlets/purl/10281045].

**Key Mentor Traits**
- **Patience**: Allow time for processing and growth.
- **Empathy**: Understand mentee's struggles and perspective.
- **Consistency**: Maintain regular communication and feedback.
- **Passion for teaching**: Enthusiasm inspires growth.

## 2. Graduated Levels of Help

### Cognitive Apprenticeship Framework

**The 6-Part Model** (Collins, Brown, Newman; applied to software development) [sources: web research on cognitive apprenticeship]

1. **Modeling** – Expert demonstrates how to perform tasks (code, debug, design), thinking aloud to make thought processes visible.
2. **Coaching** – Mentee attempts tasks while mentor observes and provides feedback, hints, or scaffolding.
3. **Scaffolding** – Expert provides temporary support, gradually removing assistance as learner gains competence.
4. **Articulation** – Learner verbalizes their thinking; expert identifies gaps or misconceptions.
5. **Reflection** – Learner compares their work with expert's, self-assesses, and learns.
6. **Exploration** – Learner tackles new problems independently, transferring skills to novel situations.

### Scaffolding Strategies [https://www.bing.com/search?q=scaffolding+learning+programming+students]

- **Task Decomposition**: Break complex problems into smaller, manageable sub-tasks.
- **Worked Examples**: Provide complete solutions with reasoning explained.
- **Questioning**: Ask probing questions (e.g., "What will happen if…?").
- **Code Templates**: Provide skeleton code; students fill in missing parts.
- **Just-in-Time Teaching**: Deliver resources/hints exactly when learners encounter difficulty.
- **Gradual Release of Responsibility**: Move from highly scaffolded → independent as confidence builds.

## 3. Diagnosing Stuck vs. Thinking

### Expert Diagnostic Signals

**When a Learner is Actively "Thinking"** [web research]
- Can articulate what they're trying next or the problem they're solving.
- Trying multiple, reasoned approaches after analyzing the problem.
- Making logical progress through strategies or code paths.
- Silent but obviously engaged in logical consideration.
- **Mentor action**: Give time and space; encourage with prompts, whiteboarding, or pseudocode for clarity.

**When a Learner is "Stuck"** [web research]
- Unable to articulate what the issue is or what to try next.
- Repeating the same attempt without meaningful change.
- Visible frustration, anxiety, or feeling mentally blocked.
- Not sure what path to take forward.
- **Mentor action**: Suggest reframing, provide scaffolding, revisit foundations, or model problem-solving.

### Questions Experts Ask to Diagnose
- "What are you trying right now?"
- "What do you think the next step is?"
- "Can you describe the issue in your own words?"
- "What don't you understand here?"

**The Key Distinction**: Productive thinking involves **strategic exploration and ability to verbalize a plan**; being stuck involves **repeated fruitless attempts and inability to articulate next steps**.

## 4. Productive Struggle vs. Unproductive Frustration

### Productive Struggle [web research]

**Definition**: Effortful process where learners grapple with concepts, make mistakes, and try different strategies without giving up.

**Signals of Productive Struggle**
- **Engaged persistence**: Actively working, trying new approaches, asking questions, using resources.
- **Curiosity**: Expressing interest in finding solutions ("What if I try this?").
- **Self-talk**: Verbalizing thinking, reflecting on what works ("I got this part, but this next step is tricky…").
- **Incremental progress**: Making slow but meaningful progress; mistakes lead to learning or new strategies.
- **Positive emotions**: Occasional frustration paired with satisfaction or excitement when progressing.
- **Duration**: Usually 5–15 minutes before breakthrough or need for help.

**Why it matters**: This is where deep, meaningful learning happens. Mistakes and iteration are essential for internalizing problem-solving strategies.

---

### Unproductive Frustration [web research]

**Definition**: Challenge far exceeds current abilities or available resources, leading to disengagement or discouragement.

**Signals of Unproductive Frustration**
- **Withdrawal**: Giving up, sitting passively, saying "I can't do this" or "I'm just not good at this."
- **Negative self-talk**: Internalized beliefs that ability is fixed ("This is impossible").
- **Emotional distress**: Visible signs (sighing, tears, anger, anxiety).
- **Off-task behavior**: Distraction, fidgeting, or avoiding the task to escape discomfort.
- **Repeated errors**: Making the same mistakes without adjusting strategy or learning from them.
- **Duration**: Persisting beyond 15–20 minutes without progress or meaningful strategy shift.

**Why it matters**: Beyond this point, learning stops and confidence erodes. Early intervention is critical.

---

### How to Distinguish Them

| **Dimension** | **Productive Struggle** | **Unproductive Frustration** |
|---|---|---|
| **Engagement** | Active exploration | Passive or withdrawn |
| **Verbalization** | Can describe approach & next step | Cannot articulate what's wrong |
| **Emotion** | Occasional frustration + satisfaction | Persistent distress, hopelessness |
| **Strategy** | Changing approaches, trying new things | Repeating same failed attempt |
| **Self-talk** | Growth-oriented ("I'm learning") | Fixed/defeatist ("I'm not smart enough") |
| **Duration** | 5–15 minutes per attempt | 15+ minutes with no progress |

**Mentor Response**
- **Productive struggle**: Encourage, ask questions, give time. "You're on the right track—what else can you try?"
- **Unproductive frustration**: Intervene! Scaffold, break into smaller tasks, model, or revisit foundations. "Let's take this one piece at a time."

## 5. Concrete 6-7 Level Graduated Help Escalation Model

### How to Use This Model

This model defines **escalation triggers** and **concrete examples** for knowing *when* to move from one help level to the next. The triggers are based on **time stuck**, **attempt count**, **learner signals**, and **engagement cues**.

---

### **LEVEL 1: Encouragement & Thinking Time**

**Description**  
Learner gets space and gentle encouragement to think independently. No specific hints yet—just supportive presence.

**When to Use**
- Learner just encountered the problem.
- They appear engaged (silent but thinking, or verbalizing a plan).
- They haven't tried anything yet OR just started.

**Example**
- Mentor: "This is a tricky one. Take your time and think through what's happening."
- Or simply: "What's your instinct here?" (gives space for thinking without direct help).

**Trigger to Escalate to Level 2**
- Learner has been silent/stuck for 3–5 minutes **and** cannot describe what they're trying next.
- OR learner explicitly asks a question.
- OR visible frustration signals appear (sighing, negative self-talk).

---

### **LEVEL 2: Socratic Questioning**

**Description**  
Mentor asks guiding questions to help learner think through the problem themselves. Questions should prompt reflection, not answers.

**When to Use**
- Learner is engaged but not yet on the right track.
- They need nudge in the right direction without being told the answer.

**Example Questions**
- "Can you describe what the error message says?"
- "What are the inputs and outputs for this function?"
- "What have you tried so far, and what happened?"
- "Is there a pattern you're noticing?"
- "What would happen if you added a print statement here?"

**Trigger to Escalate to Level 3**
- Learner cannot answer the Socratic question after 2–3 attempts.
- Learner is now showing signs of frustration (off-task behavior, negative self-talk).
- 5–10 minutes have passed with no meaningful progress.

---

### **LEVEL 3: Hint (General)**

**Description**  
Mentor provides a general, non-specific hint that points to a *category* of solution, not the solution itself.

**When to Use**
- Learner is stuck and Socratic questioning hasn't unblocked them.
- Learner needs direction but should still do the work.

**Example Hints**
- "This problem might need a loop. Have you considered what kind?"
- "You'll need to check the variable's value at this point."
- "Think about what data structure would handle multiple items."
- "Start by writing pseudocode for what you want to happen."
- "This is similar to the problem we solved earlier. Can you remember how?"

**Trigger to Escalate to Level 4**
- Learner still stuck after 3–5 minutes with hint.
- Learner asks for more specificity ("What do you mean by…?").
- Frustration increasing (time stuck: 10–15 minutes total).

---

### **LEVEL 4: Hint (Specific)**

**Description**  
Mentor gives a more specific hint that narrows the solution space significantly—pointing to a particular code location, variable, or step.

**When to Use**
- General hint wasn't enough; learner needs clearer direction.
- Learner is at risk of unproductive frustration (15+ min stuck).

**Example Hints**
- "Look at line 12. What does `x` equal at that point?"
- "You need to modify the condition in your `if` statement. What's the opposite of `True`?"
- "The bug is in how you're initializing the array. Try starting with an empty list."
- "You're close! The function exists—check the documentation for the exact parameter order."

**Trigger to Escalate to Level 5**
- Learner still confused after 3–5 minutes.
- Learner says "I don't get what you mean" or shows deep confusion.
- 15–20 minutes stuck total; frustration evident.

---

### **LEVEL 5: Partial Solution / Code Skeleton**

**Description**  
Mentor provides a partially-filled template or broken-down solution steps. Learner still fills in gaps and completes the work.

**When to Use**
- Learner needs to see *structure* to understand the path forward.
- Full answer isn't yet revealed, but trajectory is clear.
- Time/frustration threshold reached; must prevent shutdown.

**Example**
```python
# Skeleton provided to learner:
def find_max(numbers):
    max_value = ???  # Initialize to what?
    for num in numbers:
        if ???:  # What condition?
            max_value = num
    return max_value
```

Learner fills in the `???` parts.

**Trigger to Escalate to Level 6**
- Learner cannot complete skeleton even after explanation of each part.
- Learner has been working for 20+ minutes with recurring confusion.
- Need to preserve confidence and move forward.

---

### **LEVEL 6: Demonstrated Solution with Explanation**

**Description**  
Mentor writes/shows the full correct solution, then walks through it step-by-step, explaining the "why" behind each part.

**When to Use**
- Learner is at risk of shutdown (frustration critical).
- Problem is more complex than initially thought.
- Time spent justifies moving to full solution to preserve momentum and learning.
- Goal shifts from discovery to **understanding** the approach.

**Example**
```python
def find_max(numbers):
    max_value = numbers[0]  # Start with first element
    for num in numbers:     # Check each number
        if num > max_value: # If larger than current max
            max_value = num # Update max
    return max_value
```

**Explanation**:  
"We start with the first number as our 'candidate max.' Then we loop through each number and compare it. If we find a bigger one, we replace our current max. This way, we've seen all numbers and kept track of the largest."

**Trigger to Escalate to Level 7**
- Learner still confused after walkthrough and cannot explain back.
- Learner needs to **revisit foundational concepts** to proceed.
- The problem reveals a gap in earlier knowledge.

---

### **LEVEL 7: Foundation Reset + Reteach**

**Description**  
Mentor steps back and reteaches the *foundational concept* that the learner is missing. This may involve:
- Simpler related examples.
- Revisiting core definitions or syntax.
- Using multiple modalities (pseudocode, diagrams, analogies).
- Paired/coached exploration of basics.

**When to Use**
- Learner's confusion suggests a gap in prerequisite knowledge.
- Learner has struggled through Levels 1–6 without gaining clarity.
- Problem is a symptom of deeper misunderstanding.

**Example**
If learner struggles with loops despite multiple hints, mentor might:
- Revisit: "What does a `for` loop do?"
- Use analogy: "Imagine a checklist. You go through each item one by one until you reach the end. That's what a loop does."
- Write simple example: `for i in [1, 2, 3]: print(i)` — just this, no complexity.
- Use pseudocode: "Start here → Check each item → When done, exit."

**After Reset**:  
Circle back to the original problem with new understanding. May repeat Level 1–4 with better foundation.

### **Trigger Thresholds Summary**

| **Trigger** | **Escalation Rule** |
|---|---|
| **Time Stuck** | 5 min → L2; 10 min → L3; 15 min → L4; 20 min → L5; 25 min → L6; 30+ min → L7 |
| **Attempts Failed** | 1–2 attempts → stay in level; 3+ failed attempts → escalate |
| **Frustration Signals** | Sigh, negative self-talk, off-task → escalate immediately (skip levels if needed) |
| **Clarity** | Cannot articulate next step → escalate |
| **Learner Request** | Explicit "I need help" → escalate at least 1–2 levels |

## 7. Key Takeaways

1. **Good mentoring is guided discovery**: Ask questions, provide scaffolding, and let learners do the thinking. Pair programming and code reviews are powerful vehicles.

2. **Graduated help prevents both extremes**: Too much help → dependency and shallow learning. Too little → frustration and shutdown. The right escalation model finds the middle.

3. **Distinguish productive struggle from frustration**: Productive struggle has engagement, curiosity, and incremental progress. Frustration has withdrawal, repeated failures, and negative self-talk. Time and engagement are key diagnostics.

4. **The 7-level model operationalizes scaffolding**: With clear triggers (time, attempts, signals), mentors can systematically increase help while preserving learning. Each level is temporary—the goal is always to fade support.

5. **Foundation gaps surface late**: If learners cannot advance despite full solutions and explanations, revisit core concepts. Level 7 reset often reveals what's truly missing.
